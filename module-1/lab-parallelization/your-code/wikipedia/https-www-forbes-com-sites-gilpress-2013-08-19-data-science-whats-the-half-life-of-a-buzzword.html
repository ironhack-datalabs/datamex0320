Data Science: What\'s The Half-Life Of A Buzzword?

Enterprise & Cloud

Data Science Skills--Source: Drew Conway

\n \n
\n
\n
\n

A recent survey conducted by Gregory Piatetsky-Shapiro of KDnuggets, found a \xe2\x80\x9cstrong interest in online certificates and MS degrees in Analytics, Big Data, Data Mining, and Data Science, even among those who already have graduate degrees.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

According to Piatetsky\xe2\x80\x99s analysis of graduate degrees in these fields, they started to emerge in 2007, with a big spike in 2012. The proliferation of these programs--which the survey\xe2\x80\x99s findings indicate will continue unabated for the foreseeable future--prompted a strong response on Twitter from Meta Brown, a consultant and writer on business analytics, and rejoinders by Piatetsky and Shlomo Engelson Argamon, director of Illinois Institute of Technology\xe2\x80\x99s new Master in Data Science program.   Delighted, I convened via email a virtual panel on data science education.

\n

Brown didn\xe2\x80\x99t mince words: \xe2\x80\x9cMore than thirty percent of student loans are now delinquent. Over 500 schools now have student loan default rates higher than their graduation rates. I have lost count of the number of my personal acquaintances who have obtained advanced degrees with the aim of career advancement, yet found that advancement eluded them. Much of what\'s on offer these days is of poor quality, and even academically excellent programs are no assurance of concrete rewards.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n

Piatetsky disagreed, arguing that the proliferation of these programs is \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable response to industry demand\xe2\x80\x9d and predicting that \xe2\x80\x9cmarket forces will take care of the weeding out of weaker degrees.\xe2\x80\x9d Argamon, for his part, had strong words of his own: \xe2\x80\x9cThe development of data science degree programs is absolutely necessary. We all know that there is a clear need now and in the foreseeable future for many more data science professionals. These people need to have the strong statistical knowledge, software engineering ability, and communication skills which only a well-designed multidisciplinary program can provide. This distinct body of knowledge and skills cannot be conveyed by traditional educational programs such as statistics or computer science.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Indeed, there is no doubt that the proliferation of these graduate programs is driven by various estimates of the current meager supply and expected high demand in the future of people skilled at data analysis (see MGI and Gartner).  Yet Brown warns of jumping to conclusions regarding the expected return on investment on these programs and points to the lack of agreement on what constitutes data science: \xe2\x80\x9cMoney is not the only reward of education, yet it is surely the primary selling point used to market data science programs, and the primary motivator for students. But there\'s no clear definition of data science and no clear understanding of what knowledge employers are willing to pay for, or how much they will pay, now or in the future. Already I know many competent, diligent data analysts who are unemployed or underemployed. So, I am highly skeptical that the students who will invest their time and money in data science programs will reap the rewards they have been led to expect.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n
\n

Argamon agrees that \xe2\x80\x9cthere are as yet no standards for data science curriculum content\xe2\x80\x9d which leads to differences in the quality and nature of the educational experience in the programs on offer today. But unlike Brown, he finds a silver lining in the lack of consensus regarding data science and thinks that the very proliferation of these programs \xe2\x80\x9cshines the attention on data science education that will help us evolve such standards.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

There is more or less a consensus about the lack of consensus regarding a clear definition of data science. And no lack of complaints about the murky situation. See, for example, Robin Bloor\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cData Science Rant\xe2\x80\x9d--\xe2\x80\x9cThese two words, when conjoined, are utterly misleading.\xe2\x80\x9d  Bloor\xe2\x80\x99s rant is \xe2\x80\x9cphilosophical,\xe2\x80\x9d but I can share empirical evidence about both the current fuzziness and the prominence of the term. I learned from a recent Department of Defense DARPA announcement, asking for proposals to investigate \xe2\x80\x9cthe national security threat posed by public data,\xe2\x80\x9d that there are \xe2\x80\x9cprinciples of data science,\xe2\x80\x9d presumed to be understood by all potential proposers (the exact language is \xe2\x80\x9cBased on principles of data science, develop tools to characterize and assess the nature, persistence, and quality of the data\xe2\x80\x9d). Intrigued, I emailed Dr. Christopher White, the \xe2\x80\x9cTopic Author,\xe2\x80\x9d and asked what he meant by \xe2\x80\x9cPrinciples of Data Science\xe2\x80\x9d and whether he can point me to a relevant reference. His answer: \xe2\x80\x9cMy usage was general, like \xe2\x80\x98natural science.\xe2\x80\x99 There isn\xe2\x80\x99t a specific reference.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Just as the lack of clarity and established principles do not stand in the way of using \xe2\x80\x9cdata science\xe2\x80\x9d in requests for proposals, it hasn\xe2\x80\x99t slowed down the sprouting of new graduate programs with \xe2\x80\x9cdata science\xe2\x80\x9d in their names. In addition to the one at the Illinois Institute of Technology (IIT), there are, for example, the Master of Information and Data Science from UC Berkeley\xe2\x80\x99s iSchool (online only), MSc in Data Science and Management from Imperial College (London), MS in Data Science from New York University, Graduate Certificate of Advanced Studies in Data Science from Syracuse University\xe2\x80\x99s School of Information Studies, and the Certification of Professional Achievement in Data Sciences from Columbia\xe2\x80\x99s Institute for Data Sciences and Engineering.

\n

Until the launch of these programs this year, \xe2\x80\x9cBusiness Analytics\xe2\x80\x9d used to be the most popular term used in the name of these programs. I asked IIT\xe2\x80\x99s Argamon why they went with \xe2\x80\x9cdata science\xe2\x80\x9d instead.  \xe2\x80\x9c\xe2\x80\x99Data scientist\xe2\x80\x99 as a job title is significantly trending upwards,\xe2\x80\x9d Argamon says, \xe2\x80\x9cwhile \xe2\x80\x98analyst\xe2\x80\x99 is trending downwards (as is the more specific title \xe2\x80\x98data analyst\xe2\x80\x99). A second consideration (perhaps related to these trends) is that "analytics" is both too specific as well as too general a term. Data analysis is just one part of data science\xe2\x80\xa6 In our view, the term \xe2\x80\x98data science\xe2\x80\x99 more clearly delineates the field we seek to teach\xe2\x80\x94technically rigorous statistics, machine learning, and scalable computation, together with  practical training in working with data, developing meaningful visualizations, and communicating usefully with a variety of non-technical stakeholders.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Are we seeing the eclipse of a term\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cbusiness analytics\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94which became a buzzword only six or seven years ago? The sudden emergence in 2007 of graduate programs in \xe2\x80\x9cbusiness analytics\xe2\x80\x9d mentioned above can be attributed to a single paper that launched the term into buzzwordom: Tom Davenport\xe2\x80\x99s Harvard Business Review article \xe2\x80\x9cCompeting on Analytics,\xe2\x80\x9d published in January of 2006.

\n

But \xe2\x80\x9cbusiness analytics\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x94which replaced \xe2\x80\x9cdata mining\xe2\x80\x9d as the term of choice for describing the analysis of data\xe2\x80\x94was soon to be eclipsed by the rise of new players, new participants, and new tools and technologies. \xe2\x80\x9cBig Data\xe2\x80\x9d changed the landscape. The \xe2\x80\x9cold\xe2\x80\x99 guard tried for a while to jump on the new bandwagon by using \xe2\x80\x9cbig data analytics,\xe2\x80\x9d but the new players persisted and eventually took over the discussion with a new term: Data Science.

\n

In October 2012, Davenport (with DJ Patil) heralded again the dawn of a new era by publishing \xe2\x80\x9cData Scientist: The Sexiest Job of the 21st Century,\xe2\x80\x9d again in the Harvard Business Review. Davenport was also instrumental earlier in his career in the success of \xe2\x80\x9cbusiness process re-engineering\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cEnterprise Resource Planning (ERP),\xe2\x80\x9d so his ability to call a major trend and his timing are indisputable. Guided by Davenport\xe2\x80\x99s uncanny intuition (ignoring his hedging of bets with Analytics 3.0), and assuming that the rise of one buzzword signals the beginning of the decline of the one it is replacing, I conclude that the half-life of a buzzword (at least in the case of \xe2\x80\x9canalytics\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cbusiness analytics\xe2\x80\x9d) is exactly 81 months, the time that passed between the publication of Davenport\xe2\x80\x99s article on analytics and the one on data scientists.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cData Science\xe2\x80\x9d could disappear, to be eclipsed by the next buzzword. But it may stick around, just like another incongruous joining of two words, \xe2\x80\x9ccomputer science,\xe2\x80\x9d did. This will happen, I predict, only if it becomes, like computer science, an established and well-defined discipline, with its own academic departments and all the necessary paraphernalia such as professional associations, journals, conferences, and awards.
