Chapter 1. Introduction: What Is Data Science?

\n\n\n

Over the past few years, there\xe2\x80\x99s been a lot of hype in the media about \xe2\x80\x9cdata science\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cBig Data.\xe2\x80\x9d A reasonable first reaction to all of this might be some combination of skepticism and confusion; indeed we, Cathy and Rachel, had that exact reaction.

\n\n

And we let ourselves indulge in our bewilderment for a while, first separately, and then, once we met, together over many Wednesday morning breakfasts. But we couldn\xe2\x80\x99t get rid of a nagging feeling that there was something real there, perhaps something deep and profound representing a paradigm shift in our culture around data. Perhaps, we considered, it\xe2\x80\x99s even a paradigm shift that plays to our strengths. Instead of ignoring it, we decided to explore it more.

\n\n

But before we go into that, let\xe2\x80\x99s first delve into what struck us as confusing and vague\xe2\x80\x94perhaps you\xe2\x80\x99ve had similar inclinations. After that we\xe2\x80\x99ll explain what made us get past our own concerns, to the point where Rachel created a course on data science at Columbia University, Cathy blogged the course, and you\xe2\x80\x99re now reading a book based on it.

\n\n\n\n\n\n\n
\n

Big Data and Data Science Hype

\n\n

Let\xe2\x80\x99s get this out of the way right off the bat, because many of you are likely skeptical of data science already for many of the reasons we were. We want to address this up front to let you know: we\xe2\x80\x99re right there with you. If you\xe2\x80\x99re a skeptic too, it probably means you have something useful to contribute to making data science into a more legitimate field that has the power to have a positive impact on society.

\n\n

So, what is eyebrow-raising about Big Data and data science? Let\xe2\x80\x99s count the ways:

\n
    \n
  1. \n

    There\xe2\x80\x99s a lack of definitions around the most basic terminology. What is \xe2\x80\x9cBig Data\xe2\x80\x9d anyway? What does \xe2\x80\x9cdata science\xe2\x80\x9d mean? What is the relationship between Big Data and data science? Is data science the science of Big Data? Is data science only the stuff going on in companies like Google and Facebook and tech companies? Why do many people refer to Big Data as crossing disciplines (astronomy, finance, tech, etc.) and to data science as only taking place in tech? Just how big is big? Or is it just a relative term? These terms are so ambiguous, they\xe2\x80\x99re well-nigh meaningless.

    \n
    2. \n
  2. \n

    There\xe2\x80\x99s a distinct lack of respect for the researchers in academia and industry labs who have been working on this kind of stuff for years, and whose work is based on decades (in some cases, centuries) of work by statisticians, computer scientists, mathematicians, engineers, and scientists of all types. From the way the media describes it, machine learning algorithms were just invented last week and data was never \xe2\x80\x9cbig\xe2\x80\x9d until Google came along. This is simply not the case. Many of the methods and techniques we\xe2\x80\x99re using\xe2\x80\x94and the challenges we\xe2\x80\x99re facing now\xe2\x80\x94are part of the evolution of everything that\xe2\x80\x99s come before. This doesn\xe2\x80\x99t mean that there\xe2\x80\x99s not new and exciting stuff going on, but we think it\xe2\x80\x99s important to show some basic respect for everything that came before.

    \n
    3. \n
  3. \n

    The hype is crazy\xe2\x80\x94people throw around tired phrases straight out of the height of the pre-financial crisis era like \xe2\x80\x9cMasters of the Universe\xe2\x80\x9d to describe data scientists, and that doesn\xe2\x80\x99t bode well. In general, hype masks reality and increases the noise-to-signal ratio. The longer the hype goes on, the more many of us will get turned off by it, and the harder it will be to see what\xe2\x80\x99s good underneath it all, if anything.

    \n
    4. \n
  4. \n

    Statisticians already feel that they are studying and working on the \xe2\x80\x9cScience of Data.\xe2\x80\x9d That\xe2\x80\x99s their bread and butter. Maybe you, dear reader, are not a statistician and don\xe2\x80\x99t care, but imagine that for the statistician, this feels a little bit like how identity theft might feel for you. Although we will make the case that data science is not just a rebranding of statistics or machine learning but rather a field unto itself, the media often describes data science in a way that makes it sound like as if it\xe2\x80\x99s simply statistics or machine learning in the context of the tech industry.

    \n
    5. \n
  5. \n

    People have said to us, \xe2\x80\x9cAnything that has to call itself a science isn\xe2\x80\x99t.\xe2\x80\x9d Although there might be truth in there, that doesn\xe2\x80\x99t mean that the term \xe2\x80\x9cdata science\xe2\x80\x9d itself represents nothing, but of course what it represents may not be science but more of a craft.

    \n
    6. \n\n
\n
\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n
\n

Getting Past the Hype

\n\n

Rachel\xe2\x80\x99s experience going from getting a PhD in statistics to working at Google is a great example to illustrate why we thought, in spite of the aforementioned reasons to be dubious, there might be some meat in the data science sandwich. In her words:

\n
\n

It was clear to me pretty quickly that the stuff I was working on at Google was different than anything I had learned at school when I got my PhD in statistics. This is not to say that my degree was useless; far from it\xe2\x80\x94what I\xe2\x80\x99d learned in school provided a framework and way of thinking that I relied on daily, and much of the actual content provided a solid theoretical and practical foundation necessary to do my work.

\n\n

But there were also many skills I had to acquire on the job at Google that I hadn\xe2\x80\x99t learned in school. Of course, my experience is specific to me in the sense that I had a statistics background and picked up more computation, coding, and visualization skills, as well as domain expertise while at Google. Another person coming in as a computer scientist or a social scientist or a physicist would have different gaps and would fill them in accordingly. But what is important here is that, as individuals, we each had different strengths and gaps, yet we were able to solve problems by putting ourselves together into a data team well-suited to solve the data problems that came our way.

\n\n

Here\xe2\x80\x99s a reasonable response you might have to this story. It\xe2\x80\x99s a general truism that, whenever you go from school to a real job, you realize there\xe2\x80\x99s a gap between what you learned in school and what you do on the job. In other words, you were simply facing the difference between academic statistics and industry statistics.

\n\n

We have a couple replies to this:

\n\n
    \n
  • \n

    Sure, there\xe2\x80\x99s is a difference between industry and academia. But does it really have to be that way? Why do many courses in school have to be so intrinsically out of touch with reality?

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Even so, the gap doesn\xe2\x80\x99t represent simply a difference between industry statistics and academic statistics. The general experience of data scientists is that, at their job, they have access to a larger body of knowledge and methodology, as well as a process, which we now define as the data science process (details in Chapter\xc2\xa02), that has foundations in both statistics and computer science.

    \n
    • \n
\n\n

Around all the hype, in other words, there is a ring of truth: this is something new. But at the same time, it\xe2\x80\x99s a fragile, nascent idea at real risk of being rejected prematurely. For one thing, it\xe2\x80\x99s being paraded around as a magic bullet, raising unrealistic expectations that will surely be disappointed.

\n\n

Rachel gave herself the task of understanding the cultural phenomenon of data science and how others were experiencing it. She started meeting with people at Google, at startups and tech companies, and at universities, mostly from within statistics departments.

\n\n

From those meetings she started to form a clearer picture of the new thing that\xe2\x80\x99s emerging. She ultimately decided to continue the investigation by giving a course at Columbia called \xe2\x80\x9cIntroduction to Data Science,\xe2\x80\x9d which Cathy covered on her blog. We figured that by the end of the semester, we, and hopefully the students, would know what all this actually meant. And now, with this book, we hope to do the same for many more people.

\n
\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n
\n

Why Now?

\n\n

We have massive amounts of data about many aspects of our lives, and, simultaneously, an abundance of inexpensive computing power. Shopping, communicating, reading news, listening to music, searching for information, expressing our opinions\xe2\x80\x94all this is being tracked online, as most people know.

\n\n

What people might not know is that the \xe2\x80\x9cdatafication\xe2\x80\x9d of our offline behavior has started as well, mirroring the online data collection revolution (more on this later). Put the two together, and there\xe2\x80\x99s a lot to learn about our behavior and, by extension, who we are as a species.

\n\n

It\xe2\x80\x99s not just Internet data, though\xe2\x80\x94it\xe2\x80\x99s finance, the medical industry, pharmaceuticals, bioinformatics, social welfare, government, education, retail, and the list goes on. There is a growing influence of data in most sectors and most industries. In some cases, the amount of data collected might be enough to be considered \xe2\x80\x9cbig\xe2\x80\x9d (more on this in the next chapter); in other cases, it\xe2\x80\x99s not.

\n\n

But it\xe2\x80\x99s not only the massiveness that makes all this new data interesting (or poses challenges). It\xe2\x80\x99s that the data itself, often in real time, becomes the building blocks of data products. On the Internet, this means Amazon recommendation systems, friend recommendations on Facebook, film and music recommendations, and so on. In finance, this means credit ratings, trading algorithms, and models. In education, this is starting to mean dynamic personalized learning and assessments coming out of places like Knewton and Khan Academy. In government, this means policies based on data.

\n\n

We\xe2\x80\x99re witnessing the beginning of a massive, culturally saturated feedback loop where our behavior changes the product and the product changes our behavior. Technology makes this possible: infrastructure for large-scale data processing, increased memory, and bandwidth, as well as a cultural acceptance of technology in the fabric of our lives. This wasn\xe2\x80\x99t true a decade ago.

\n\n

Considering the impact of this feedback loop, we should start thinking seriously about how it\xe2\x80\x99s being conducted, along with the ethical and technical responsibilities for the people responsible for the process. One goal of this book is a first stab at that conversation.

\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n
\n

Datafication

\n\n

In the May/June 2013 issue of Foreign Affairs, Kenneth Neil Cukier and Viktor Mayer-Schoenberger wrote an article called \xe2\x80\x9cThe Rise of Big Data\xe2\x80\x9d. In it they discuss the concept of datafication, and their example is how we quantify friendships with \xe2\x80\x9clikes\xe2\x80\x9d: it\xe2\x80\x99s the way everything we do, online or otherwise, ends up recorded for later examination in someone\xe2\x80\x99s data storage units. Or maybe multiple storage units, and maybe also for sale.

\n\n

They define datafication as a process of \xe2\x80\x9ctaking all aspects of life and turning them into data.\xe2\x80\x9d As examples, they mention that \xe2\x80\x9cGoogle\xe2\x80\x99s augmented-reality glasses datafy the gaze. Twitter datafies stray thoughts. LinkedIn datafies professional networks.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n\n

Datafication is an interesting concept and led us to consider its importance with respect to people\xe2\x80\x99s intentions about sharing their own data. We are being datafied, or rather our actions are, and when we \xe2\x80\x9clike\xe2\x80\x9d someone or something online, we are intending to be datafied, or at least we should expect to be. But when we merely browse the Web, we are unintentionally, or at least passively, being datafied through cookies that we might or might not be aware of. And when we walk around in a store, or even on the street, we are being datafied in a completely unintentional way, via sensors, cameras, or Google glasses.

\n\n

This spectrum of intentionality ranges from us gleefully taking part in a social media experiment we are proud of, to all-out surveillance and stalking. But it\xe2\x80\x99s all datafication. Our intentions may run the gamut, but the results don\xe2\x80\x99t.

\n\n

They follow up their definition in the article with a line that speaks volumes about their perspective:

\n
\n

Once we datafy things, we can transform their purpose and turn the information into new forms of value.

\n\n

Here\xe2\x80\x99s an important question that we will come back to throughout the book: who is \xe2\x80\x9cwe\xe2\x80\x9d in that case? What kinds of value do they refer to? Mostly, given their examples, the \xe2\x80\x9cwe\xe2\x80\x9d is the modelers and entrepreneurs making money from getting people to buy stuff, and the \xe2\x80\x9cvalue\xe2\x80\x9d translates into something like increased efficiency through automation.

\n\n

If we want to think bigger, if we want our \xe2\x80\x9cwe\xe2\x80\x9d to refer to people in general, we\xe2\x80\x99ll be swimming against the tide.

\n
\n\n\n\n\n\n
\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n
\n

The Current Landscape (with a Little History)

\n\n

So, what is data science? Is it new, or is it just statistics or analytics rebranded? Is it real, or is it pure hype? And if it\xe2\x80\x99s new and if it\xe2\x80\x99s real, what does that mean?

\n\n

This is an ongoing discussion, but one way to understand what\xe2\x80\x99s going on in this industry is to look online and see what current discussions are taking place. This doesn\xe2\x80\x99t necessarily tell us what data science is, but it at least tells us what other people think it is, or how they\xe2\x80\x99re perceiving it. For example, on Quora there\xe2\x80\x99s a discussion from 2010 about \xe2\x80\x9cWhat is Data Science?\xe2\x80\x9d and here\xe2\x80\x99s Metamarket CEO Mike Driscoll\xe2\x80\x99s answer:

\n
\n

Data science, as it\xe2\x80\x99s practiced, is a blend of Red-Bull-fueled hacking and espresso-inspired statistics.

\n\n

But data science is not merely hacking\xe2\x80\x94because when hackers finish debugging their Bash one-liners and Pig scripts, few of them care about non-Euclidean distance metrics.

\n\n

And data science is not merely statistics, because when statisticians finish theorizing the perfect model, few could read a tab-delimited file into R if their job depended on it.

\n\n

Data science is the civil engineering of data. Its acolytes possess a practical knowledge of tools and materials, coupled with a theoretical understanding of what\xe2\x80\x99s possible.

\n\n

Driscoll then refers to Drew Conway\xe2\x80\x99s Venn diagram of data science from 2010, shown in Figure\xc2\xa01-1.

\n\n\n\n

He also mentions the sexy skills of data geeks from Nathan Yau\xe2\x80\x99s 2009 post, \xe2\x80\x9cRise of the Data Scientist\xe2\x80\x9d, which include:

\n\n
    \n
  • \n

    Statistics (traditional analysis you\xe2\x80\x99re used to thinking about)

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Data munging (parsing, scraping, and formatting data)

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Visualization (graphs, tools, etc.)

    \n
    • \n
\n\n

But wait, is data science just a bag of tricks? Or is it the logical extension of other fields like statistics and machine learning?

\n\n

For one argument, see Cosma Shalizi\xe2\x80\x99s posts here and here, and Cathy\xe2\x80\x99s posts here and here, which constitute an ongoing discussion of the difference between a statistician and a data scientist. Cosma basically argues that any statistics department worth its salt does all the stuff in the descriptions of data science that he sees, and therefore data science is just a rebranding and unwelcome takeover of statistics.

\n\n

For a slightly different perspective, see ASA President Nancy Geller\xe2\x80\x99s 2011 Amstat News article, \xe2\x80\x9cDon\xe2\x80\x99t shun the \xe2\x80\x98S\xe2\x80\x99 word\xe2\x80\x9d, in which she defends statistics:

\n
\n

We need to tell people that Statisticians are the ones who make sense of the data deluge occurring in science, engineering, and medicine; that statistics provides methods for data analysis in all fields, from art history to zoology; that it is exciting to be a Statistician in the 21st century because of the many challenges brought about by the data explosion in all of these fields.

\n\n

Though we get her point\xe2\x80\x94the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cart history to zoology\xe2\x80\x9d is supposed to represent the concept of A to Z\xe2\x80\x94she\xe2\x80\x99s kind of shooting herself in the foot with these examples because they don\xe2\x80\x99t correspond to the high-tech world where much of the data explosion is coming from. Much of the development of the field is happening in industry, not academia. That is, there are people with the job title data scientist in companies, but no professors of data science in academia. (Though this may be changing.)

\n\n

Not long ago, DJ Patil described how he and Jeff Hammerbacher\xe2\x80\x94then at LinkedIn and Facebook, respectively\xe2\x80\x94coined the term \xe2\x80\x9cdata scientist\xe2\x80\x9d in 2008. So that is when \xe2\x80\x9cdata scientist\xe2\x80\x9d emerged as a job title. (Wikipedia finally gained an entry on data science in 2012.)

\n\n

It makes sense to us that once the skill set required to thrive at Google\xe2\x80\x94working with a team on problems that required a hybrid skill set of stats and computer science paired with personal characteristics including curiosity and persistence\xe2\x80\x94spread to other Silicon Valley tech companies, it required a new job title. Once it became a pattern, it deserved a name. And once it got a name, everyone and their mother wanted to be one. It got even worse when Harvard Business Review declared data scientist to be the \xe2\x80\x9cSexiest Job of the 21st Century\xe2\x80\x9d.

\n
\n
The Role of the Social Scientist in Data Science
\n

Both LinkedIn and Facebook are social network companies. Oftentimes a description or definition of data scientist includes hybrid statistician, software engineer, and social scientist. This made sense in the context of companies where the product was a social product and still makes sense when we\xe2\x80\x99re dealing with human or user behavior. But if you think about Drew Conway\xe2\x80\x99s Venn diagram, data science problems cross disciplines\xe2\x80\x94that\xe2\x80\x99s what the substantive expertise is referring to.

\n\n

In other words, it depends on the context of the problems you\xe2\x80\x99re trying to solve. If they\xe2\x80\x99re social science-y problems like friend recommendations or people you know or user segmentation, then by all means, bring on the social scientist! Social scientists also do tend to be good question askers and have other good investigative qualities, so a social scientist who also has the quantitative and programming chops makes a great data scientist.

\n\n

But it\xe2\x80\x99s almost a \xe2\x80\x9chistorical\xe2\x80\x9d (historical is in quotes because 2008 isn\xe2\x80\x99t that long ago) artifact to limit your conception of a data scientist to someone who works only with online user behavior data. There\xe2\x80\x99s another emerging field out there called computational social sciences, which could be thought of as a subset of data science.

\n
\n\n

But we can go back even further. In 2001, William Cleveland wrote a position paper about data science called \xe2\x80\x9cData Science: An action plan to expand the field of statistics.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n\n

So data science existed before data scientists? Is this semantics, or does it make sense?

\n\n

This all begs a few questions: can you define data science by what data scientists do? Who gets to define the field, anyway? There\xe2\x80\x99s lots of buzz and hype\xe2\x80\x94does the media get to define it, or should we rely on the practitioners, the self-appointed data scientists? Or is there some actual authority? Let\xe2\x80\x99s leave these as open questions for now, though we will return to them throughout the book.

\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n
\n

Data Science Jobs

\n\n

Columbia just decided to start an Institute for Data Sciences and Engineering with Bloomberg\xe2\x80\x99s help. There are 465 job openings in New York City alone for data scientists last time we checked. That\xe2\x80\x99s a lot. So even if data science isn\xe2\x80\x99t a real field, it has real jobs.

\n\n

And here\xe2\x80\x99s one thing we noticed about most of the job descriptions: they ask data scientists to be experts in computer science, statistics, communication, data visualization, and to have extensive domain expertise. Nobody is an expert in everything, which is why it makes more sense to create teams of people who have different profiles and different expertise\xe2\x80\x94together, as a team, they can specialize in all those things. We\xe2\x80\x99ll talk about this more after we look at the composite set of skills in demand for today\xe2\x80\x99s data scientists.

\n

As we mentioned earlier, a data science team works best when different skills (profiles) are represented across different people, because nobody is good at everything. It makes us wonder if it might be more worthwhile to define a \xe2\x80\x9cdata science team\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94as shown in Figure\xc2\xa01-3\xe2\x80\x94than to define a data scientist.

Thought Experiment: Meta-Definition

\n\n

Every class had at least one thought experiment that the students discussed in groups. Most of the thought experiments were very open-ended, and the intention was to provoke discussion about a wide variety of topics related to data science. For the first class, the initial thought experiment was: can we use data science to define data science?

\n\n

The class broke into small groups to think about and discuss this question. Here are a few interesting things that emerged from those conversations:

\n
\n
Start with a text-mining model.
\n
\n

We could do a Google search for \xe2\x80\x9cdata science\xe2\x80\x9d and perform a text-mining model. But that would depend on us being a usagist rather than a prescriptionist with respect to language. A usagist would let the masses define data science (where \xe2\x80\x9cthe masses\xe2\x80\x9d refers to whatever Google\xe2\x80\x99s search engine finds). Would it be better to be a prescriptionist and refer to an authority such as the Oxford English Dictionary? Unfortunately, the OED probably doesn\xe2\x80\x99t have an entry yet, and we don\xe2\x80\x99t have time to wait for it. Let\xe2\x80\x99s agree that there\xe2\x80\x99s a spectrum, that one authority doesn\xe2\x80\x99t feel right, and that \xe2\x80\x9cthe masses\xe2\x80\x9d doesn\xe2\x80\x99t either.

\n
\n
So what about a clustering algorithm?
\n
\n

How about we look at practitioners of data science and see how they describe what they do (maybe in a word cloud for starters)? Then we can look at how people who claim to be other things like statisticians or physicists or economists describe what they do. From there, we can try to use a clustering algorithm (which we\xe2\x80\x99ll use in Chapter\xc2\xa03) or some other model and see if, when it gets as input \xe2\x80\x9cthe stuff someone does,\xe2\x80\x9d it gives a good prediction on what field that person is in.

\n
\n
\n\n

Just for comparison, check out what Harlan Harris recently did related to the field of data science: he took a survey and used clustering to define subfields of data science, which gave rise to Figure\xc2\xa01-4.

\n\n\n
\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n
\n

OK, So What Is a Data Scientist, Really?

\n\n

Perhaps the most concrete approach is to define data science is by its usage\xe2\x80\x94e.g., what data scientists get paid to do. With that as motivation, we\xe2\x80\x99ll describe what data scientists do. And we\xe2\x80\x99ll cheat a bit by talking first about data scientists in academia.

\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n
\n

In Academia

\n\n

The reality is that currently, no one calls themselves a data scientist in academia, except to take on a secondary title for the sake of being a part of a \xe2\x80\x9cdata science institute\xe2\x80\x9d at a university, or for applying for a grant that supplies money for data science research.

\n\n

Instead, let\xe2\x80\x99s ask a related question: who in academia plans to become a data scientist? There were 60 students in the Intro to Data Science class at Columbia. When Rachel proposed the course, she assumed the makeup of the students would mainly be statisticians, applied mathematicians, and computer scientists. Actually, though, it ended up being those people plus sociologists, journalists, political scientists, biomedical informatics students, students from NYC government agencies and nonprofits related to social welfare, someone from the architecture school, others from environmental engineering, pure mathematicians, business marketing students, and students who already worked as data scientists. They were all interested in figuring out ways to solve important problems, often of social value, with data.

\n\n

For the term \xe2\x80\x9cdata science\xe2\x80\x9d to catch on in academia at the level of the faculty, and as a primary title, the research area needs to be more formally defined. Note there is already a rich set of problems that could translate into many PhD theses.

\n\n

Here\xe2\x80\x99s a stab at what this could look like: an academic data scientist is a scientist, trained in anything from social science to biology, who works with large amounts of data, and must grapple with computational problems posed by the structure, size, messiness, and the complexity and nature of the data, while simultaneously solving a real-world problem.

\n\n

The case for articulating it like this is as follows: across academic disciplines, the computational and deep data problems have major commonalities. If researchers across departments join forces, they can solve multiple real-world problems from different domains.

\n
\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n
\n

In Industry

\n\n

What do data scientists look like in industry? It depends on the level of seniority and whether you\xe2\x80\x99re talking about the Internet/online industry in particular. The role of data scientist need not be exclusive to the tech world, but that\xe2\x80\x99s where the term originated; so for the purposes of the conversation, let us say what it means there.

\n\n

A chief data scientist should be setting the data strategy of the company, which involves a variety of things: setting everything up from the engineering and infrastructure for collecting data and logging, to privacy concerns, to deciding what data will be user-facing, how data is going to be used to make decisions, and how it\xe2\x80\x99s going to be built back into the product. She should manage a team of engineers, scientists, and analysts and should communicate with leadership across the company, including the CEO, CTO, and product leadership. She\xe2\x80\x99ll also be concerned with patenting innovative solutions and setting research goals.

\n\n

More generally, a data scientist is someone who knows how to extract meaning from and interpret data, which requires both tools and methods from statistics and machine learning, as well as being human. She spends a lot of time in the process of collecting, cleaning, and munging data, because data is never clean. This process requires persistence, statistics, and software engineering skills\xe2\x80\x94skills that are also necessary for understanding biases in the data, and for debugging logging output from code.

\n\n

Once she gets the data into shape, a crucial part is exploratory data analysis, which combines visualization and data sense. She\xe2\x80\x99ll find patterns, build models, and algorithms\xe2\x80\x94some with the intention of understanding product usage and the overall health of the product, and others to serve as prototypes that ultimately get baked back into the product. She may design experiments, and she is a critical part of data-driven decision making. She\xe2\x80\x99ll communicate with team members, engineers, and leadership in clear language and with data visualizations so that even if her colleagues are not immersed in the data themselves, they will understand the implications.

\n\n

That\xe2\x80\x99s the high-level picture, and this book is about helping you understand the vast majority of it. We\xe2\x80\x99re done with talking about data science; let\xe2\x80\x99s go ahead and do some!
